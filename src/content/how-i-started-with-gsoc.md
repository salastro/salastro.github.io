Sometime during 2025, on what seemed like just another ordinary day, fate decided to intervene in its mysterious way. During a casual conversation, a friend of mine mentioned GSoC to me. Until that moment, I had never heard of it before, nor did I know about the opportunities it offered. Curious, I started looking into it, and the more I learned, the more interested I became. The idea of contributing to open-source projects and collaborating with experienced developers felt both exciting and inspiring. So, naturally, I decided to take a chance and apply.

Back then, I was interested in two organizations: [neovim](https://neovim.io/) and [sympy](https://www.sympy.org/). I have been using NeoVIM for years at this point, and even though I did not feel confident at the least in contributing to the codebase, after all all that I have done was tinkering with my configuration, I decided to give it a shot. On the other hand, I have been using SymPy extensively for the last couple of months; it had helped me with coursework, projects, and most importantly my internship at the time. I felt more confident in contributing to SymPy as their codebase is in Python, which I am very experienced at.

I read through proposed ideas, previous projects, documentations, and mailing lists. The neovim community experience was awful. As they are very dismissive, arrogant, stubborn, and simply annoying. I will not elaborate on this point as I do not want this post to become an attack against one of my favourite projects of all time—I am in fact using it right now to write this article.

The SymPy community was more graceful; they replied nicely and clarified their stances on my inquiries. That said, sometimes they were not responsive; I could hear the sound of crickets in the mailing list. Still, despite the occasional silence, I kept exploring the project. I began reading through issues tagged as “good first issue,” studying the structure of the repository, and trying to understand how the development workflow operated. At first, everything felt overwhelming. SymPy is a massive project with years of accumulated abstractions, conventions, and mathematical machinery. Even something as seemingly simple as fixing a small bug often required understanding layers of symbolic manipulation logic that were far beyond anything I had written before.

I applied to both organizations and got rejected by both. I do wholeheartedly believe that I did deserve this as at the time I did not posses enough skills and experience to preform well on any of the projects I wanted to work on. I was not affected much by the rejections as I did realize my abilities—or lack thereof. I was totally turned off by the Neovim community, so I decided to focus my mind on the SymPy work.

I made a number of pull requests targeting an [issue](https://github.com/sympy/sympy/issues/27586) that I felt comfortable tackling, symbolic integration of some special functions involving `erf`. It all came from one issue and I kept solving one problem after another related to it. In total, I made [8 PRs](https://github.com/sympy/sympy/pulls?q=is%3Apr+author%3Asalastro+created%3A%3C2026-05-12+is%3Amerged) that got merged into the main code. At the time, I did not hope for any recongition.

After that, I applied again, with a [stronger proposal](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1P-9y4KBoq9HPbwKfGgkrxeWuBuXWzvwk/view?usp=sharing) this time. I was no longer approaching the application process as someone merely fascinated by the idea of open source. I now had actual experience interacting with the codebase, reading other people’s patches, debugging difficult issues, and contributing features that real users would eventually rely on. More importantly, I had become comfortable with being uncomfortable. During my first attempt, every unfamiliar abstraction felt intimidating; now, confusion had simply become part of the process. Instead of being discouraged by not understanding something immediately, I learned to slowly untangle it piece by piece.

Ironically, getting rejected the first time turned out to be one of the most valuable parts of the entire experience. Had I been accepted immediately, I would probably have approached the project with far less maturity and far less technical understanding. The rejection forced me to confront the gap between what I thought I knew and what I actually knew. More importantly, it pushed me to keep contributing even when there was no guarantee of reward waiting at the end.

Days went by and finally, I got accepted into the program. It was a surreal moment. All the hard work, the late nights debugging, the countless pull requests, and the emotional rollercoaster of rejection and perseverance had finally paid off. I felt a deep sense of accomplishment and gratitude. I was now part of a global community of developers, working together to build something meaningful. The journey had been challenging, but it had also been incredibly rewarding. I had not only gained technical skills but also learned valuable lessons about resilience, humility, and the power of community.
